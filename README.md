Been chewing on your question since we spoke. Here's roughly where I've landed. Still rough, and I'd rather argue it out in person, but this is the gist.
The thing that keeps striking me is that nobody actually owns this value chain top to bottom. Even the US leans on Taiwan for the actual chips and on one Dutch company, ASML, for the machines that print the advanced ones. There's literally one firm on earth that makes those, and China's been blocked from buying them. So the chain is split across a handful of countries, and if you own even one piece nobody can work around, you get real leverage over players far bigger than you. Worth noting India just got ASML access through Tata that China's been denied.
Which is why I don't think the goal is to build the whole stack ourselves. We'd lose that on money alone. But I also don't buy the other popular answer, that India just builds AI apps on top of everyone else's chips and models. That's a tenant's position, no leverage, and you're exposed the day someone upstream decides to squeeze you. I'd rather we own a few pieces that are genuinely hard to replace, and use them as leverage.
A few places I think we can actually do that, with stuff already happening:
Chip design. We already have something like a fifth of the world's chip designers, so this is a talent game, not a spending one. RISC-V is the interesting angle, it's open, so no paying Arm or Intel for permission, and you can build your own IP on it. Mindgrove, InCore, Calligo, Netrasemi are all already at it.
Packaging. Less glamorous, but as chips stop getting smaller, how you package and stack them is where the gains come from now, and it's a fraction of the cost of a cutting-edge fab. Micron and Kaynes already have this running in Gujarat, Tata's building a big one in Assam.
Power chips (SiC, GaN). AI's real constraint is turning into power, not just compute, and these chips don't need cutting-edge fabs, so we're not behind. AGNIT and SicSem are early here. This is also where it overlaps with the storage stuff from the assignment.
Indian-language models. Not trying to out-build GPT, that's a money fight. But the Indian-language and Indian-data layer is something no US or Chinese lab is going to do well. Sarvam's now a unicorn, trained its models here from scratch, and there's BharatGen, Gnani, AI4Bharat. Honest caveat: it all still runs on Nvidia, which is exactly why the harder pieces above matter.
Energy for compute. You can't run sovereign AI without your own power, and this is the one area where India's genuinely ahead, given the renewables and storage build-out that's already going. The AI data centers going up (L&T, Yotta) need exactly that.
One thing I want to flag on purpose: I'm keeping the big players, Tata, Micron, CG Power, L&T, Reliance, in the picture alongside the startups, and that's deliberate. The expensive backbone here, the fabs, the packaging plants, the gigawatt data centers, only gets built by balance sheets that size, and they're already doing it. They're the ones with the capex to actually put India on the map. The startup bets don't replace them, they ride on top of what the big guys are financing. For a fund like yours, honestly, the incumbents doing the heavy lifting is what makes the smaller bets less risky.
So if I had to boil it down: back the pieces where money alone can't win, design talent, RISC-V IP, packaging, language and data, energy, and let the conglomerates pour the concrete. That's more or less what you said to me on the call anyway, find the thing with a moat that ten times the cash can't cross.
